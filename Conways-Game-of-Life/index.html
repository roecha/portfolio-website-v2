Conway's Game of Life

About

John Conway's Game of Life Conway's Game of Life is a simple and fascinating simulation. Imagine a grid where each cell can be alive or dead. The game progresses in steps, or "generations." In each generation, the fate of a cell is determined by its neighbors. If a live cell has too few or too many live neighbors, it dies. If a dead cell has exactly three live neighbors, it comes to life. The patterns that emerge can be surprisingly intricate and unpredictable, often resembling living organisms despite being just a set of rules acting on a grid. It's not a traditional game with players, but rather a captivating way to observe how simple rules can create complex behaviors.

Rules

  1. A cell is "alive" when it is black
  2. A live cell with less than 2 neighbors will die due to underpopulation
  3. A live cell with two or three neighbors will live to the next generation
  4. A live cell with greater than 3 neighbors will die due to overpopulation
  5. A dead cell with exactly three neighbors will become alive due to reproduction

Info

  • Grid size: the width and height of the grid
  • Interval time: the length of time in each generation
  • ctl+click to fill in a full row
  • alt+click to fill in a full column
  • If the evolutions start to slow down, try clicking away from the grid and they should go back to normal speed
  • Make the window smaller to zoom out on the grid
  • You may add pieces to the grid while it is running
